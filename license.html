"Why should I care?" RollCall is free software. But, why is that so special? "I use Windows and Chrome, and they aren't free software," you say.

I would encourage you to watch Richard Stallman's talk at TEDxGeneva in 2014. Here's some more food for thought:

Well, it comes down to your freedoms and a guarantee of privacy. How do you know that Google hasn't put a backdoor into Chrome, allowing anyone at Google to see exactly what you're doing? How do you know that Microsoft hasn't put a backdoor into Windows, allowing them to take control into your computer? (happens) The truth is, you don't. But, when you use free software, people can do (and have done) extensive reviews to figure out whether people have, or even just if someone's made a mistake.

Additionally, when you use free software, you're gaining the freedom to make changes to it, and even redistribute those changes to other people (as long as you tell them it's been changed). Richard Stallman, founder of the Free Software Foundation, often tells a story about the printers at MIT. The software for the printers was free software. Since printing often took quite a while, they modified them so it would tell you on your terminal when your printing job was done. Then, they got new printers that were not free software, so they couldn't make any modifications; they lost out, because MIT had chosen proprietary printer software over free software.

These days, the term "open source" is often used to mean "free software". However, "open source" has another meaning, which is that you distribute the source along with your product, but do not allow people to modify or redistribute it. This is good in some cases, but for the most part not as useful. When we say free software, we mean free as in freedom.
