Twenty years ago, privacy was a big concern for computer systems. But now (possibly thanks to a massive propaganda campaign by the American government and media), few people truly care about privacy (or say they do, at least). Here's an extreme example. In many books (including George Orwell's 1984, the pinnacle of this genre), a society is depicted in which many fundamental freedoms really do not exist. One of these freedoms that is violated almost 100% of the time, and is often the first to go, is privacy. In these societies without privacy, any expressions of disagreement with the government is frowned upon, if not illegal. This is used as a means of information supression; if anyone attempts to rally support against the government, the authorities know instantly, thanks to the spying; then, the dissident is labeled a terrorist, threat to national security, etc., and supressed. When you let one freedom fall, the others fall like dominoes; right now, privacy is under the most threat, yet is most important. Measures that RollCall takes Although I can't go into too many details without getting very technical, here's an overview:
  • Messages are encrypted using a hybrid RSA/AES architecture. A quick overview of how RSA works: Alice wants to send a message to Bob, so she encrypts the messsage with Bob's public key. The only person who can read it, at that point, is Bob; not even Alice can read it after she's encrypted it. Bob then decrypts it with his private key. (If anyone discovered his private key, then they could read the message, too, so it is imperative that the private key is secured). Also, AES is just a symmetric key encryption scheme; you use the same key to encrypt it as decrypt it.
    When Charlie sends a message to Denis using RollCall, it contains the AES key, encrypted using RSA with Denis's public key, and the message, encrypted with the AES key (uniquely generated for each message). When Denis goes to decrypt it, he first goes to the AES key, and decrypts it using his RSA private key, and the decrypts the message with the RSA key he just decrypted.
    Fun fact: RSA stands for Rivest-Shamir-Adleman, the three inventors of the system.
  • For calls, we rely on the encryption that is required by the WebRTC standard. Recent versions of Firefox are signifigantly than Chromium (Chrome and Opera).
    Here's the technical description of how Firefox is better: Firefox, starting from Firefox 38, requires the use of Perfect Forward Secrecy, in the form of Diffie-Hellman key exchange or Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman key exchange. This means that the private key is generated uniquely every time, so if it is compromized, it only compromizes one session (as opposed to with RSA, where it compromizes every session).
